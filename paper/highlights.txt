Highlights

- A nine-channel structural lifecycle model combining six rational channels (Social Security, bequests, medical expenditure risk with health-mortality correlation, survival pessimism, pricing loads, inflation erosion, long-term care risk) with two preference channels (age-varying consumption needs, state-dependent utility) predicts US voluntary annuity ownership of 1.7%, plausibly close to two HRS empirical comparators (2.02% lifetime contract indicator, 95% CI [1.68%, 2.43%]; 3.34% any-annuity income proxy, 95% CI [2.89%, 3.85%]); the structural baseline underpredicts by roughly 0.3-1.6 pp
- A complementary reduced-form transport applies the UK 2015 pension-freedoms reform as out-of-sample variation: applying the UK post-reform retention (17% mid; bracket [13%, 25%]) to the frictionless population benchmark of 41.85% yields predicted US ownership of 7.5% (bracket [5.7%, 11.0%]); the two models reach the same neighborhood from independent directions
- An exact Shapley decomposition over all 1,024 channel subsets attributes ownership change without order dependence; two behavioral channels (source-dependent utility, purchase-event disutility) enter with large but offsetting Shapley values (-31.1 pp and +44.4 pp respectively), indicating operation on distinct decision margins rather than redundant parameterization of the same wedge
- Two distinct policy levers operate on different margins: a supply-side reform (group pricing) raises predicted ownership to 5.8%, and a demand-side reform (default architecture, psi_purchase = 0) reaches 63.4%
- The paper does not claim to resolve the puzzle; standard mechanisms account for most of the Yaari-to-observed gap, leaving a small residual that is consistent with the literature-magnitude behavioral channels but is not moment-matched to US data
