# Dissolving the Annuity Puzzle: A Critical Survey

## Abstract

Yaari (1965) proved that a rational consumer facing an uncertain lifetime, with no bequest motive and access to actuarially fair annuities, should annuitize all wealth. The gap between this prediction and observed behavior -- voluntary immediate annuity ownership rates of 3-6 percent across developed economies -- has been labeled the "annuity puzzle." This survey argues that the aggregate puzzle, as conventionally framed for classic immediate life annuities against the Yaari benchmark, should be retired. The full-annuitization result rests on nine restrictive assumptions, each empirically false. Pre-existing annuitization through Social Security and defined-benefit pensions places the typical retiree at 50-75 percent annuitization before any voluntary purchase. Bequest motives, health expenditure risk, and stochastic mortality each substantially reduce predicted demand in calibrated lifecycle models. Under prospect theory with standard parameters and narrow framing, predicted annuity demand can fall to zero, though the result is sensitive to reference point specification and the framing assumption. Supply-side frictions reduce the welfare-equivalent value of available products well below actuarial fair value. Cross-cultural evidence is broadly consistent with low voluntary demand across diverse institutional contexts. Multiple channels combine to predict approximately what is observed, though important questions remain -- particularly regarding deferred income annuities and the heterogeneous welfare question of for which households additional annuitization would improve welfare.

**Keywords:** annuity puzzle; life-cycle theory; longevity risk; bequest motives; prospect theory; money's worth ratio

**JEL Classification:** D15, D81, D91, G22, H55, J26

## 1. Introduction

Yaari (1965) proved that a consumer facing an uncertain lifetime, possessing no bequest motive, and with access to actuarially fair life annuities should allocate all wealth to annuity contracts. Annuities dominate conventional assets because they offer a mortality credit -- a return premium financed by redistributing wealth from those who die to survivors. Davidoff, Brown, and Diamond (2005) subsequently generalized the result under weaker preference assumptions.

The empirical reality is starkly different. Voluntary life annuity purchases are vanishingly rare across developed economies. In the United States, approximately 3.6 percent of single retirees aged 65 to 69 hold privately purchased income annuities (Lockwood, 2012). In Australia, lifetime annuity products account for a negligible fraction of AUD 3.6 trillion in superannuation assets (Australian Treasury, 2023). When the United Kingdom's 2015 pension freedoms removed compulsory annuitization, sales collapsed by approximately 75 percent (Financial Conduct Authority, 2018). Germany's Riester pension system and Singapore's CPF LIFE both encountered significant resistance.

Throughout this survey, "annuity demand" refers specifically to voluntary purchase of classic immediate life annuities -- products converting a lump sum into an irrevocable, lifelong income stream. Deferred income annuities and hybrid products raise distinct issues discussed in Section 8. Measurement varies across studies (ownership rates, wealth shares, flow measures), but by any measure voluntary annuitization is low relative to the Yaari benchmark.

The chasm between theory and observation was labeled the "annuity puzzle" and became one of the most durable research programs in household finance. The term itself signals an interpretive commitment: the theoretical prediction is taken as the benchmark, and observed behavior is treated as the anomaly requiring explanation. This framing has generated a vast literature in which hundreds of papers propose partial explanations -- ranging from adverse selection and loading costs to bequest motives and cognitive limitations -- each typically modeled as an independent friction that reduces but does not eliminate optimal annuity demand. The cumulative impression left by this literature is that the puzzle has been chipped away at its margins while its core remains intact, that some residual gap persists between what rational agents should do and what households actually do.

This paper argues that the cumulative impression is wrong. Six decades of research have shown that the puzzle of low demand for classic immediate life annuities substantially dissolves once the Yaari framework's assumptions are replaced with empirically grounded alternatives. The contribution is not new data or a new model, but a demonstration that the existing literature, taken as a whole, provides multiple empirically plausible mechanisms that can rationalize the low levels of voluntary SPIA demand observed across developed economies, and that no leading framework robustly predicts high take-up once realistic institutions and preferences are incorporated. The gap between theory and evidence is primarily a gap between a fragile theoretical benchmark and the realities of human preferences, institutional constraints, and insurance markets. This dissolution is a positive finding: multiple models predict low demand under realistic parameterizations. The normative question -- whether non-annuitization reduces welfare for specific subpopulations -- is analytically distinct and remains open (Sections 5.6 and 8.2). The appropriate response is to retire the Yaari-benchmark puzzle framing and redirect research toward the heterogeneous welfare question: for which households, under what conditions, would additional annuitization improve welfare?

The case proceeds along several interconnected lines. Section 2 demonstrates that the full-annuitization result rests on a conjunction of nine restrictive assumptions, each empirically false, and shows that the result is fragile: relaxing individual assumptions in calibrated models can drive predicted demand to zero. Section 3 shows that pre-existing annuitization places the typical retiree at 50-75 percent annuitized, and that calibrated bequest motives reduce predicted ownership to 4-5 percent, matching the observed rate. Section 4 shows how health expenditure risk raises the value of liquidity and how correlated health-mortality risk (Reichling and Smetters, 2015) can drive optimal demand to zero. Section 5 demonstrates that under prospect theory with standard parameter values, predicted demand falls to zero, and that framing effects produce preference swings incompatible with the expected utility foundations of the Yaari result. Section 6 examines supply-side failures and cross-cultural evidence consistent with low voluntary demand across diverse institutional contexts. Section 7 synthesizes these channels, showing that their interaction effects are multiplicative rather than additive.

This paper is situated relative to prior surveys and related synthesis work. Brown (2007) provided an early overview emphasizing behavioral factors. Benartzi, Previtero, and Thaler (2011) offered a compact treatment focusing on framing effects and default options. Pashchenko (2013) developed a structural accounting framework decomposing the contributions of various channels. The present survey differs in four respects: it integrates channels that prior surveys treated in isolation, highlighting multiplicative interaction effects; it incorporates cross-cultural evidence postdating the earlier surveys, notably the UK's 2015 pension freedoms and Australia's superannuation drawdown patterns; it introduces evolutionary psychology arguments as complementary support for bequest motives; and it argues explicitly for retiring the aggregate puzzle framing while identifying the heterogeneous welfare question as the more productive successor research program.

Methodologically, this paper constitutes a theory-based critical survey in the sense encompassed by the Journal of Economic Surveys. It does not present new empirical results or develop a novel theoretical model. Rather, it assesses the role of specific theoretical assumptions within the annuity puzzle literature, evaluates the empirical plausibility of each, and synthesizes findings from across behavioral economics, household finance, insurance economics, public economics, evolutionary psychology, and comparative institutional analysis. The synthetic approach is essential because the force of the argument is cumulative: no single channel is new, but the demonstration that multiple channels jointly rationalize low observed demand requires bringing them together within a unified analytical framework. The paper concludes by proposing that the organizing question of this research program should be reformulated: not "why do consumers fail to annuitize?" but rather "what level of annuitization is optimal given realistic preferences, constraints, and institutions, and how does it compare with observed behavior?"

The literature surveyed spans Yaari (1965) to 2025, assembled through keyword searches in EconLit, Google Scholar, RePEc/IDEAS, SSRN, and NBER Working Papers using terms including "annuity puzzle," "annuitization," "life annuity demand," "longevity insurance," and "money's worth annuity," supplemented by citation chasing from canonical anchors (Yaari 1965; Davidoff, Brown, and Diamond 2005; Mitchell et al. 1999; Lockwood 2012; Brown 2007). The survey is selective: studies were included based on their contribution to the nine Yaari benchmark assumptions (Section 2) and to the principal channels of dissolution. Cross-cultural evidence is drawn from seven institutional contexts (UK, US, Australia, Singapore, Germany, Netherlands, Chile); Scandinavian countries are acknowledged as relevant but not treated in depth.

## 2. The Yaari Framework: Assumptions, Extensions, and Fragility

The entire annuity puzzle rests on a single theoretical result. Yaari (1965) proved that under specific conditions, a rational consumer facing an uncertain lifespan should convert all wealth into actuarially fair life annuities. This section demonstrates that the full-annuitization result rests on restrictive assumptions that, when replaced with empirically grounded alternatives, substantially reduce or eliminate predicted annuity demand.

### 2.1 The Original Yaari (1965) Model

Yaari (1965) considers a consumer who lives from time zero to a random death time *T*, distributed according to a known survival function, and who maximizes expected discounted lifetime utility over an uncertain horizon. Without annuities, the consumer holds conventional bonds earning a riskless rate *r*, and the Euler equation governing optimal consumption growth contains the mortality hazard rate as an additional discount factor. The consumer is penalized for the risk of dying with unspent resources.

With actuarially fair annuities, the picture changes dramatically. The annuity pays a return equal to the bond rate plus the mortality hazard rate -- the mortality credit -- financed by redistributing wealth from those who die to survivors. This credit exactly offsets the mortality discount in the Euler equation, so the consumer's optimal consumption path becomes identical to that of a consumer with a certain lifetime. Since the annuity return strictly exceeds the bond return wherever the mortality hazard is positive, annuities stochastically dominate bonds for any consumer who derives no utility from wealth at death. Full annuitization is optimal.

The result explicitly assumes: (i) expected utility maximization; (ii) additively separable utility; (iii) no bequest motive; (iv) actuarially fair pricing; (v) a known, fixed survival function; and (vi) exponential discounting. None has survived empirical scrutiny. Expected utility is contradicted by loss aversion and probability distortion (Kahneman and Tversky, 1979). Additive separability is rejected by evidence of habit formation (Constantinides, 1990) and state-dependent marginal utility (Finkelstein, Luttmer, and Notowidigdo, 2013). Roughly 75 percent of elderly Americans report bequest motives (Kopczuk and Lupton, 2007). Annuities offer only 80-85 cents of expected payouts per premium dollar (Mitchell et al., 1999). Survival functions shift stochastically with health shocks (Reichling and Smetters, 2015). And present bias challenges exponential discounting (Laibson, 1997).

### 2.2 The Nine Assumptions and Their Empirical Status

Yaari's explicit assumptions do not exhaust the assumptions embedded in the full-annuitization benchmark. His model embeds additional assumptions that subsequent researchers identified as independently important. First, Yaari's consumer begins with a lump sum and receives no annuity income from other sources -- an assumption of no pre-existing annuitized income. In reality, Social Security and DB pensions annuitize 50-75 percent of median retiree wealth (Dushi and Webb, 2004; Poterba, Venti, and Wise, 2011). Second, the only source of uncertainty is the date of death; the consumer faces no health expenditure shocks. De Nardi, French, and Jones (2010) showed that medical expense risk is a key driver of precautionary saving among the elderly. Third, the consumer can trade in a complete annuity market spanning the full space of state-contingent claims (Davidoff, Brown, and Diamond, 2005). Fourth, additive separability embeds state-independent utility, contradicted by evidence that the marginal utility of consumption declines with health deterioration (Finkelstein, Luttmer, and Notowidigdo, 2013).

Each assumption represents a dimension along which the full-annuitization result can break down. Davidoff, Brown, and Diamond (2005) showed that some of Yaari's preference assumptions (1, 2, and 6) can be relaxed if complete markets are assumed, but as Section 2.3 discusses, this shifts the load-bearing assumption rather than eliminating it. For any real-world retiree -- who holds bequest motives, receives Social Security, faces health expenditure risk, and evaluates decisions with reference-dependent preferences -- multiple assumptions are violated simultaneously, and the theoretical case for full annuitization collapses accordingly.

### 2.3 The Davidoff, Brown, and Diamond (2005) Generalization

Davidoff, Brown, and Diamond (2005) showed that with no bequest motive, complete markets, and a return premium net of costs, full annuitization is optimal regardless of utility function -- under complete markets, any consumption plan achievable without annuities can be replicated with annuities at lower cost.

This is a significant result: DBD established that full annuitization is robust to preference structure, conditional on complete markets. The preference assumptions relaxed represent a genuine generalization. However, the assumption retained and elevated -- complete markets -- is among the most counterfactual in economics. Complete markets require that consumers can trade in state-contingent claims spanning every relevant source of uncertainty: longevity, health expenditure, inflation, long-term care costs, and more. Real annuity markets offer a narrow menu of products, predominantly fixed nominal annuities, spanning only the longevity dimension. The generalization shifted the load-bearing assumption from several modestly unrealistic conditions to a single, dramatically unrealistic one. When markets are incomplete, the budget-set dominance argument fails because some consumption plans are achievable only through non-annuitized wealth. Davidoff, Brown, and Diamond also established a weaker result -- that some positive level of annuitization is welfare-improving under broader conditions -- consistent with this article's position that annuities are not valueless, but that the prediction of full annuitization is an artifact of extreme assumptions.

### 2.4 The Knife-Edge Characterization

Perturbation along any of the nine dimensions reduces optimal annuitization; along several -- bequest motives, stochastic health-mortality correlation, prospect-theoretic preferences -- calibrated models predict demand at or near zero. Calibrated bequest motives drive willingness to pay for loaded annuities to zero (Lockwood, 2012). Calibrated health-mortality-expenditure dynamics drive optimal demand to zero or negative, even without bequests and with fair pricing (Reichling and Smetters, 2015). Under cumulative prospect theory with standard parameters and narrow framing, predicted demand falls to zero, though the result is sensitive to reference point specification and the loss aversion coefficient (Hu and Scott, 2007). Replacing separable utility with the multiplicative specification of Bommier and Le Grand (2014), combined with any positive bequest motive, inverts the result: greater risk aversion produces lower annuity demand.

These perturbations operate independently; in reality, they operate simultaneously. A typical retiree holds bequest motives, receives Social Security, faces stochastic health expenditure risk, evaluates decisions with reference-dependent preferences, and confronts products priced 15-20 percent above actuarial fair value. The set of parameter configurations at which full annuitization remains optimal is vanishingly small relative to the space of empirically plausible calibrations -- every calibrated model reviewed in Sections 3-6, with the partial exception of Peijnenburg et al. (2016), produces demand well below the Yaari benchmark. The full-annuitization result is knife-edge in this specific sense: it holds only at the conjunction of multiple counterfactual assumptions, a region that is negligibly small relative to the empirically plausible parameter space. This characterization is central to the survey's argument: the annuity "puzzle" is the gap between a fragile theoretical prediction and robust empirical reality. When the prediction rests on nine restrictive assumptions that are each empirically false, the appropriate inference is that the model is inadequate as a descriptive benchmark, not that consumer behavior is anomalous. The remaining sections demonstrate this proposition channel by channel, with the interaction effects documented in Section 7 showing that the channels compound multiplicatively rather than additively.

## 3. Demand-Side Resolution I: Pre-Existing Annuitization and Bequest Motives

Once the analysis incorporates the annuitized wealth that households already possess through Social Security and defined-benefit pensions, and once it accounts for the widespread preference to leave wealth to descendants, predicted voluntary private annuity demand converges toward observed levels. The puzzle, on these two grounds alone, is substantially attenuated.

### 3.1 The accounting error: pre-existing annuitization

The standard methodology for documenting the annuity puzzle compares private annuity wealth to financial wealth, understating the degree to which retirees are already insured against longevity risk. The relevant object is the share of total retirement resources already in life-contingent form -- including Social Security and defined-benefit pensions, which are lifelong and largely non-commutable (Social Security is inflation-indexed by statute and non-commutable; DB pensions vary, with many providing limited or no cost-of-living adjustment, and some plans offering lump-sum distribution options). To measure "annuitization" while excluding them is analogous to measuring housing consumption while ignoring the house in which the household lives.

The quantitative significance is large. Lockwood (2012) reports that households in the bottom quartile have approximately 75 percent of total wealth already annuitized; at the median, approximately 67 percent; even at the 80th-90th percentiles, roughly half. Only in the top decile does the share fall to approximately 33 percent. Private annuities constitute roughly one percent of total wealth among Americans aged 65 and older (Johnson, Burman, and Kobes, 2004). Dushi and Webb (2004) confirmed that "much of the failure of the average currently retired household to annuitize can be attributed to the exceptionally high proportions of the wealth of these cohorts that is pre-annuitized." Lockwood (2012) concurs: "people in the lower half of the wealth distribution gain little from annuities even without bequest motives because they are fairly well annuitized by Social Security and employer pensions." Bernheim (1991) found that increases in Social Security benefits raised life insurance holdings while depressing private annuity demand, and Pashchenko (2013) confirms in a structural model that once Social Security is accounted for, optimal additional private annuitization drops substantially.

Because Social Security benefits are progressive and lower-wealth households hold a larger share in Social Security form, the annuitized share tends to be inversely related to financial wealth. This compositional gradient is one contributor among several to the wealth pattern in annuity demand; differential mortality by socioeconomic status (higher-income groups live longer, partially offsetting formula progressivity), variation in claiming behavior, and differences in DB pension coverage also shape the distribution. Poterba, Venti, and Wise (2011) reinforce this point by documenting the dominance of Social Security and DB pensions in the overall wealth portfolios of retirees across the wealth distribution. An important caveat: these estimates reflect cohorts with substantial DB pension coverage. As private-sector DB coverage declines -- currently approximately 14 percent of private-industry workers in the United States have access to a defined-benefit plan -- the pre-annuitization share for future retirees will be lower, resting principally on Social Security alone (Section 8.4). The residual question concerns the top 10-20 percent: why do the wealthiest retirees not annuitize more? As the next subsection demonstrates, bequest motives provide a compelling answer.

### 3.2 Bequest motives

Yaari's (1965) theorem assumes the consumer places zero value on wealth remaining at death. Relaxing this single assumption proves sufficient to align theoretical predictions with observed behavior.

The empirical prevalence of bequest motives is well established. Kopczuk and Lupton (2007) estimate that approximately 75 percent of elderly single households report a bequest motive, and that roughly half of net wealth transferred at death reflects intentional preferences rather than accidental residuals. Among those with children, 67 percent report that leaving an inheritance is important; even among those without children, 55 percent express a desire to leave bequests (Lockwood, 2012).

The quantitative impact on annuity demand is dramatic but requires disaggregation across two margins. Lockwood (2012) estimates a lifecycle model calibrated to U.S. data. Without bequest motives, the model predicts 61 percent ownership among single retirees aged 65-69, with willingness to pay (WTP) of 25.3 percent of non-annuity wealth for actuarially fair contracts. With moderate bequest motives -- calibrated to imply bequests of roughly one-fifth of wealth -- WTP for fair annuities collapses to 3.7 percent, and WTP for annuities carrying a 10 percent load falls to zero. The empirical ownership rate among single retirees aged 65 to 69 is approximately 3.6 percent.

Two distinct objects are at work. On the intensive margin (WTP for annuity access), bequest motives alone produce a dramatic reduction robust across specifications. On the extensive margin (predicted ownership rates), the match to observed data depends not only on bequest parameterization but also on additional market frictions. In Lockwood's simulations, moderate bequest motives without a fixed purchase cost typically reduce predicted ownership to approximately 10-15 percent; it is the addition of a fixed purchase cost (approximately $2,000) that pulls predicted ownership to the 4-5 percent range matching the observed 3.6 percent. With the stronger bequest parameterization of Ameriks, Caplin, Laufer, and Van Nieuwerburgh (2011), predicted ownership falls to zero. The directional effect -- that bequest motives dramatically reduce predicted annuitization -- is robust across specifications, though the precise quantitative match depends on the specific bequest parameterization employed.

The mechanism is revealing. Lockwood's (2012) decomposition shows that in the no-bequest model, more than 75 percent of the welfare gains from annuitization derive from reclassifying accidental bequests as "waste." When the consumer places no value on wealth at death, every dollar left behind represents foregone consumption, and annuities convert this waste into spending. But once bequests enter the utility function, wealth left to heirs is a valued outcome. The mortality credit, which appears as free money in the no-bequest framework, is more accurately described as the price of forfeiting a valued bequest. With even moderate preferences, this price exceeds the benefit.

The interaction between bequest motives and annuity pricing loads further strengthens the result. Davidoff, Brown, and Diamond (2005) showed that under actuarially fair pricing, even consumers with moderate bequest motives should annuitize some wealth. But private annuities carry loads of approximately 15-20 percent against population mortality tables (Mitchell et al., 1999). Lockwood (2012) demonstrates that bequest motives and realistic loads are multiplicative rather than additive in suppressing demand: consumers whose bequest preferences alone would leave them indifferent to fair annuities are driven decisively away once the load is introduced. Kotlikoff and Spivak (1981) show that married couples can achieve approximately 70 percent of the welfare gains from fair annuity markets through intra-household risk sharing, further reducing residual demand.

### 3.3 Evolutionary foundations of bequest motives

A complementary perspective from evolutionary biology helps explain why kin-directed investment motives are so widespread. Hamilton's (1964) theory of inclusive fitness establishes that natural selection favors behaviors benefiting genetic relatives when the fitness benefit to the recipient (B), weighted by genetic relatedness (r), exceeds the fitness cost to the actor (C): rB > C. The rule describes selection pressures on heritable behavioral tendencies, not a conscious decision calculus. Evidence from small-scale societies and human life-history research documents extensive downward resource transfers across the lifespan (Hooper, Gurven, Winking, and Kaplan, 2015). Aktipis and Kurzban (2004) argue that Homo economicus "is a species that could never have evolved in the first place," because natural selection shaped human psychology for kin-directed altruism and reciprocal cooperation.

Three distinctions prevent overextension of the argument. First, inclusive fitness predicts widespread kin-directed investment, but the fitness-optimal transfer is often inter vivos rather than at death; the specific form of at-death financial bequest is culturally contingent. Evolutionary theory predicts the motivational substrate, not a specific bequest utility function (Cosmides and Tooby, 1994; Hodgson, 2004). Second, intergenerational material wealth transfer varies across economic systems: Borgerhoff Mulder et al. (2009) document substantial material transmission in pastoral and agricultural societies but limited material wealth transmission in foraging populations, where investment is primarily through embodied capital -- skills, health, and caregiving (Kaplan et al., 2000). Third, the inclusive fitness argument is strongest for biological offspring; bequests to non-biological heirs require alternative mechanisms.

The evolutionary perspective thus explains the persistence of kin-directed investment motives, making zero-bequest assumptions implausible as a descriptive foundation, without implying that a specific bequest utility function is a hardwired universal. In modern settings, inheritance law, tax policy, and financial-product design strongly mediate how kin-directed motives are expressed.

## 4. Demand-Side Resolution II: Health Expenditure Risk and Stochastic Mortality

This section identifies a third channel: the risk of catastrophic health and long-term care expenditures. Annuities convert liquid wealth into an illiquid income stream, yet retirees face medical costs that are large, unpredictable, and concentrated in states where liquidity is most valuable.

### 4.1 The liquidity imperative

The scale of health expenditure risk in retirement is difficult to overstate. Jones, De Nardi, French, McGee, and Kirschner (2018) estimate that mean annual medical expenditures (combined out-of-pocket and Medicaid, in 2014 dollars) rise from approximately $5,100 at age 70 to $29,700 by age 100; out-of-pocket expenditures alone average approximately $4,200 at age 70, with Medicaid covering the remainder. The 95th percentile of combined annual expenditures reaches approximately $111,200 at age 100. The liquidity threat to asset-holding households -- the primary candidates for voluntary annuity purchase -- is concentrated in out-of-pocket costs, since Medicaid payments accrue only after asset spend-down, a path that most potential annuity purchasers seek to avoid. The distribution is heavily right-skewed: while most retirees face manageable costs, a substantial minority confront expenses that would exhaust all but the largest portfolios.

An important institutional distinction bears on this analysis. Medicare covers the majority of acute care expenditures for Americans aged 65 and older; the dominant uninsured tail risk is long-term care -- nursing home stays, assisted living, and home health care -- which Medicare covers only for short post-acute episodes. The median annual cost of a semi-private nursing facility room was $111,325 in 2024 (Genworth, 2024), and approximately 9 percent of persons in the 2020 cohort turning 65 were projected to spend five or more years in a nursing home (Spillman and Lubitz, 2002). These costs are lumpy, unpredictable, and positively correlated with deteriorating health -- precisely the profile for which liquid wealth is most valuable and annuitized income least adequate.

### 4.2 Precautionary saving and public care aversion

The structural literature identifies medical expense risk as a key driver of precautionary wealth accumulation in retirement. De Nardi, French, and Jones (2010) find that medical expense risk is a key driver of wealth holding among the elderly -- the slow decumulation that the standard lifecycle model without health risk cannot explain.

Ameriks, Caplin, Laufer, and Van Nieuwerburgh (2011), using strategic survey methods on a sample of Vanguard clients to separate bequest motives from "public care aversion" -- the desire to avoid reliance on Medicaid-funded care -- find that both motives are economically significant. Public care aversion is widespread and particularly helps explain low spending among middle-class retirees, while bequest motives remain quantitatively important across the wealth distribution. Retirees retain liquid wealth not because they fail to understand longevity insurance, but because they rationally prefer to maintain the capacity to pay for high-quality care.

The Medicaid interaction reinforces this behavior through a mechanism related to, but not identical to, the implicit tax Brown and Finkelstein (2008) documented for long-term care insurance. Brown and Finkelstein estimate that Medicaid imposes an implicit tax of 60-75 percent on private LTC insurance for median-wealth individuals, because private benefits paid in the Medicaid-eligible state partially substitute for public payments. The mapping to annuity demand differs because annuity payouts are not contingent on needing care; nevertheless, means-tested public LTC coverage affects the value of annuitization through both eligibility and post-eligibility channels. First, annuitizing converts liquid wealth into an irrevocable payment stream, reducing the option value of holding spendable assets to self-fund high-quality care and thereby avoid (or delay) Medicaid reliance. Second, conditional on entering Medicaid-financed nursing home care, most income -- including annuity income, net of small allowances -- is typically applied to the cost of care, so the marginal value of annuity payouts in the high-expense state can be low because they effectively reduce Medicaid's payment obligation. These effects are heterogeneous, depending on household structure (single versus married under community spouse rules), annuity contract features, and state-specific Medicaid treatment of annuities and income. While some households can use Medicaid-compliant immediate annuities as part of crisis planning, the broader implication for the annuity puzzle literature is that means-tested public LTC coverage lowers the marginal value of annuitized income in precisely the states where spending flexibility is most valuable, reinforcing the public care aversion that Ameriks et al. (2011) identify as a primary driver of precautionary wealth retention.

### 4.3 Stochastic mortality and the Reichling-Smetters result

Reichling and Smetters (2015) provide the most powerful formulation. They allow health shocks to alter survival probabilities stochastically: a negative health shock simultaneously increases the marginal utility of wealth (through elevated medical costs) and reduces the remaining present value of annuity payments (through shortened life expectancy). The key mechanism is the correlation structure: the annuity becomes "valuation-risky" because a health shock generates implicit depreciation in annuitized wealth exactly when liquid resources are most valuable.

This correlation-driven mechanism -- not generic "medical expense risk" -- undermines the core value proposition of annuitization. Reichling and Smetters explicitly note that lumpy expenditures uncorrelated with health have no impact on optimal annuitization in their framework, and that in some specifications medical expense risk can even increase annuity demand (because longevity insurance covers old-age states when health costs are highest); the demand-suppressing result depends specifically on the joint distribution of health shocks, survival probabilities, and medical costs. The mortality credit is largest for individuals who live longest, but those who live longest are, on average, those who remain healthy and face lower medical costs. When this health-mortality-cost correlation is calibrated to realistic magnitudes, optimal annuity demand falls to zero or even becomes negative -- consumers may optimally wish to hold negative annuity positions (via life insurance) to hedge the joint risk of health deterioration and shortened lifespan.

This result is particularly significant because it operates even under actuarially fair pricing and even absent bequest motives -- conditions under which the Yaari result and its Davidoff, Brown, and Diamond (2005) generalization both predict full annuitization. The case for annuitization depends not only on the existence of longevity risk, which annuities address, but on the absence of correlated health-expenditure risk, which annuities exacerbate. The Reichling-Smetters result is thus not merely another friction reducing demand at the margin; it identifies a structural channel through which the core insurance value proposition of annuitization is undermined.

Peijnenburg, Nijman, and Werker (2016), examining multiple frictions jointly in a calibrated lifecycle model, find that full annuitization remains approximately optimal even after incorporating incomplete markets, background risk, bequest motives, and default risk -- leading the authors to title their paper "The Annuity Puzzle Remains a Puzzle." The divergence with this survey is methodological: Peijnenburg et al. evaluate channels within a single unified model, while the present survey synthesizes across modeling frameworks. The Peijnenburg et al. finding that no single channel suffices reinforces the emphasis on multiplicative interaction effects documented in Section 7.

## 5. Behavioral Models: From Expected Utility to Prospect Theory

The preceding sections relaxed the Yaari framework's assumptions within the expected utility paradigm. This section asks what happens when the behavioral model itself is replaced. Under standard behavioral parameterizations, predicted annuity demand can fall to zero.

### 5.1 Framing Effects and Description Invariance

Brown, Kling, Mullainathan, and Wrobel (2008) demonstrated that description invariance -- a foundational EU axiom -- fails dramatically in the annuity context. Approximately 72 percent of respondents preferred the annuity when framed as consumption insurance, versus only 21 percent when framed as an investment. The 51-percentage-point swing is strong evidence against description invariance in stated annuitization preferences. Brown et al. employed hypothetical choices, and external validity for a decision this consequential warrants caution. Nevertheless, the magnitude -- consistent across subsequent designs and demographic groups (Agnew et al., 2008; Beshears et al., 2014) -- establishes that the frame materially influences stated preferences.

The theoretical significance is substantial. If preferences shift by a factor of more than three depending on how the choice is represented, then low observed demand is difficult to reconcile with stable underlying preferences of the kind expected utility theory presupposes. Demand is, to a considerable degree, constructed by the frame -- and in many real-world settings, the frame is the investment frame. Retirees face accumulated lump sums in defined-contribution accounts and are asked whether to surrender them for an income stream. Industry materials emphasize internal rates of return and breakeven ages, coding the annuity as a bet rather than as insurance.

### 5.2 Prospect Theory and Predicted Annuity Demand

The framing results find a natural theoretical home in prospect theory. Kahneman and Tversky (1979) proposed that individuals evaluate risky prospects not in terms of final wealth levels but as gains and losses relative to a reference point. The value function is concave over gains and convex over losses, steeper for losses than for equivalent gains (loss aversion), and applied not to objective probabilities but to decision weights generated by a nonlinear probability weighting function. In its cumulative form (Tversky and Kahneman, 1992), the model is parameterized by a loss aversion coefficient of approximately 2.25, a value function exponent of 0.88, and probability weighting functions that overweight small probabilities and underweight moderate-to-large ones.

Hu and Scott (2007) were the first to apply this framework formally to the annuity decision. Their central analytical object is a reservation price -- the maximum fraction of actuarially fair cost that a CPT agent would be willing to pay for an annuity. Reservation prices below 1 imply that the annuity is unattractive even at actuarially fair pricing. Under the full CPT specification with standard parameters and narrow framing (evaluating the annuity as a standalone gamble), reservation prices for a 65-year-old male fall to approximately 0.77, implying zero demand at fair or loaded prices. Three mechanisms reinforce: loss aversion weights early-death losses at 2.25 times equivalent longevity gains; probability weighting overweights the small probability of dying soon after purchase while underweighting the moderate probability of surviving into the late eighties; and diminishing sensitivity compresses gains from extreme longevity.

Three sources of sensitivity condition this result. First, the loss aversion coefficient varies substantially across studies, with estimates typically ranging from 1.5 to 2.5 though specification-dependent estimates can be considerably higher, and probability weighting curvature also varies. Second, the reference point specification is the most consequential modeling choice: Hu and Scott assume narrow framing (evaluating the annuity as a standalone gamble); under broader integration with background wealth, the zero result need not hold. Third, with no time discounting, reservation prices can exceed 1, making annuities attractive even for CPT agents. The zero-demand result is most robust under narrow framing, moderate-to-high discounting, and standard parameters simultaneously. An additional caveat is that CPT was developed for single-period gambles; Hu and Scott's application extends it to a multiperiod consumption stream, and as they note, their results should be interpreted qualitatively given the cognitive demands of intertemporal CPT evaluation.

The qualitative finding -- that prospect theory substantially reduces predicted annuity demand relative to expected utility -- holds across a wide range of plausible parameter values, even if the specific zero result depends on the baseline parameterization and framing assumptions. The finding represents a substantial shift from the Yaari result, obtained not by layering additional frictions onto the expected utility framework but by replacing one model of decision-making with another that better fits decades of experimental evidence on choice under risk. What Hu and Scott's analysis most clearly establishes is not that "prospect theory implies zero annuitization" as a general proposition, but that under narrow framing and standard experimental parameters, CPT agents find actuarially fair annuities unattractive -- a result whose field relevance depends on whether the narrow-framing assumption accurately describes how retirees evaluate annuity decisions in practice.

Brown, Kapteyn, Luttmer, and Mitchell (2017) provided complementary evidence using a large-sample survey administered through the American Life Panel. They found that the prices at which individuals are willing to buy annuities are substantially below those at which they are willing to sell them -- a buy-sell spread inconsistent with coherent expected-utility valuation but consistent with reference-dependent evaluation, though the authors emphasize cognitive constraints on annuity valuation as the primary mechanism. Under EU, the maximum willingness to pay and minimum willingness to accept should converge; the persistent gap implies that the annuity is simultaneously valued below its cost as a purchase (coded as a loss of liquid wealth) and above its cost as an existing endowment (coded as a loss of income if surrendered). This buy-sell asymmetry is consistent with the reference-dependence mechanism that Hu and Scott (2007) modeled theoretically, though the authors emphasize cognitive constraints on annuity valuation as a primary interpretation.

### 5.3 Prospect Theory Explains Observed Market Patterns

Gottlieb (2012, working paper) argues that a single CPT framework can account for three features that EU models find difficult to generate simultaneously: the prevalence of guarantee and period-certain features that cap downside loss from early death; simultaneous holding of life insurance and annuities (under narrow framing, each is evaluated as a separate gamble satisfying loss-limiting preferences); and persistent consumer preference for loss-limiting riders over pure life annuities offering higher payments. Prospect theory offers a parsimonious descriptive account of these patterns, though EU alternatives (bequests, lifecycle needs, incomplete markets) can explain some individually.

### 5.4 Survival Pessimism

O'Dea and Sturrock (2023) documented that individuals systematically underestimate their own survival probabilities -- by 15 to 20 percentage points for those under 70 and by as much as 25 to 30 percentage points among those in their early sixties estimating survival to age 75. Using a lifecycle model calibrated to English Longitudinal Study of Ageing (ELSA) data, they show that subjective survival pessimism substantially reduces perceived annuity value: when individuals believe they are unlikely to survive to advanced ages, the expected present value of annuity payments -- as perceived by the decision-maker -- falls well below the premium, making non-purchase appear rational from the individual's perspective. This channel operates within the EU framework with distorted inputs rather than distorted preferences, and is analytically distinct from the CPT mechanisms in Section 5.2. Groneck, Ludwig, and Zimper (2016) reach a complementary conclusion using ambiguous survival beliefs, finding that pessimism about longevity can substantially reduce annuity demand using a generalized expected utility framework with non-additive probability beliefs.

### 5.5 Bounded Rationality and Complexity

The annuity purchase is among the most computationally demanding decisions a household faces: it requires estimating one's remaining lifespan distribution, understanding discount rates and the time value of money, evaluating actuarial fairness, accounting for inflation erosion over decades, integrating the annuity with existing income sources, comparing across product structures and providers, and assessing insurer solvency. Each task individually exceeds the demonstrated financial capabilities of the median retiree (Lusardi and Mitchell, 2011); collectively, they describe an optimization problem that would challenge a professional actuary. Complexity interacts with status-quo bias to compound demand suppression (Samuelson and Zeckhauser, 1988). In most institutional settings, the status quo is to retain the lump sum, and there is no institutional nudge toward annuitization comparable to the automatic enrollment that has transformed 401(k) participation rates (Madrian and Shea, 2001).

### 5.6 The Descriptive-Normative Distinction

The behavioral findings carry an important caveat. Prospect theory, framing effects, and bounded rationality are descriptive models, not normative endorsements. Loss aversion is a psychological regularity, not a welfare criterion. As Bernheim and Rangel (2009) argued, frame-dependent preferences cannot straightforwardly serve as the basis for welfare analysis. Chetty (2015) offers a complementary pragmatic approach: using behavioral evidence to bound welfare-relevant parameters without requiring full commitment to a contested structural model, a methodology well-suited to the heterogeneous welfare question identified in Section 8.2.

This paper's normative claims therefore rest on the structural channels documented in Sections 3 and 4. The welfare criterion implicitly employed is expected utility with empirically calibrated parameters -- the framework of Lockwood (2012) and Reichling and Smetters (2015) -- under which realistic bequest motives, pre-existing annuitization, health expenditure risk, and unfair pricing independently justify low annuitization for a rational agent. The behavioral evidence plays a confirmatory rather than load-bearing role. CPT-predicted choices should accordingly be described as "predicted demand under CPT" rather than "optimal annuitization."

The convergence of structural and behavioral predictions -- both classes of models predict low voluntary annuitization -- strengthens the positive case that the aggregate annuity puzzle is dissolved. However, convergence in predicted choices does not establish convergence in welfare implications. Two points of caution are essential. First, if rational low take-up is driven by high loadings, inflation risk, and illiquidity, the welfare implication is "current products are inadequate" -- a market design failure that could in principle be remedied. If behavioral low take-up is driven by loss aversion or narrow framing, the welfare implication may instead be that better framing, defaults, or simplified products could improve outcomes. These mechanisms can coexist, and observing that both push toward low annuitization does not identify which dominates welfare. Second, in a second-best world, low annuitization can be simultaneously individually rational given the existing product menu and evidence of large welfare losses from market design failures -- the absence of adequately priced real annuities, the lack of annuity-LTC bundles, or regulation-induced pricing distortions. Low annuitization may not be a "consumer mistake" and yet remain a welfare problem in the form of unaddressed insurance-market incompleteness.

The convergence argument is most compelling for households with significant pre-existing annuitization, operative bequest motives, or meaningful health expenditure risk -- characteristics that describe the majority of retirees. For the subset lacking these characteristics (single, childless, healthy, wealthy, with low bequest motives), the structural channels provide weaker justification for non-annuitization, and for such households, behavioral factors may account for welfare-reducing under-annuitization. Whether this subpopulation is large enough to constitute a policy-relevant concern is an empirical question addressed further in Section 8.2. Aggregate take-up approximately matching a model's prediction does not establish allocative efficiency at the individual level: some households may be biased toward annuitization while others are biased away, with offsetting errors producing an aggregate that appears "about right." The distinction between the aggregate positive finding and the heterogeneous normative question is central to this paper's argument.

## 6. Supply-Side Failures and Cross-Cultural Evidence

This section turns to the supply side. The annuity in Yaari's model -- actuarially fair, inflation-indexed, default-free, and frictionless -- bears little resemblance to the annuity available in real markets.

### 6.1 Pricing and Money's Worth

The foundational empirical study of annuity pricing remains Mitchell, Poterba, Warshawsky, and Brown (1999). Using actual quotes from the US individual annuity market and discounting projected payment streams at Treasury yields, they report money's-worth ratios (MWRs) -- the expected present value of payouts per premium dollar -- of roughly 0.80-0.85 against population mortality tables and roughly 0.90-0.94 against annuitant mortality tables. Mitchell et al. (1999, p. 1310) report that for a 65-year-old male, the gap between population-table and annuitant-table MWRs is approximately 11 cents -- "roughly half" of the total shortfall -- reflecting the longer lives of voluntary annuity purchasers relative to the general population. The residual shortfall of the annuitant-table MWR from unity reflects insurer expenses, statutory reserves, distribution costs, taxes, and profit. Both components vary meaningfully by age, sex, and assumed discount rate. Wettstein, Munnell, Hou, and Gok (2021) found this overall pricing wedge remarkably stable over nearly three decades, with the population-table MWR fluctuating between 0.76 and 0.87 for men during 2001-2019. They also documented a growing disparity across socioeconomic groups: by 2019, the MWR gap between top- and bottom-education-tercile white men had grown to 14 cents, implying that less-educated, lower-income buyers face substantially lower money's worth under uniform pricing that does not reflect their shorter average lifespans.

The MWR picture is sensitive to the interest rate environment. The 2022-2024 rate cycle substantially improved SPIA payout rates. Total annuity industry sales reached record levels, driven primarily by fixed and fixed-indexed products. However, voluntary SPIA and longevity annuity purchases did not increase commensurately -- reinforcing the thesis that demand suppression reflects structural preferences rather than pricing alone.

Adverse selection compounds the pricing problem. Finkelstein and Poterba (2002), using the UK's dual voluntary-compulsory market structure, demonstrated that adverse selection is roughly twice as severe in voluntary markets as in compulsory markets. Finkelstein and Poterba (2004) showed that selection operates along contract structure dimensions -- particularly back-loading and estate-payment features -- but found little to no selection along payment size, the variable that standard adverse selection models emphasize. The authors caution that "it is unlikely that this adverse selection, and the associated high effective prices for annuities, can fully explain the limited demand for voluntary annuities" (Finkelstein and Poterba, 2004, p. 186). The consequence is a self-reinforcing equilibrium: prices are set for a pool dominated by long-lived individuals, average-mortality buyers rationally decline to purchase, and the pool becomes yet more adversely selected.

A conceptual distinction is important. The MWR is a pricing metric: the expected present value of nominal payouts per premium dollar, conditional on a specified mortality table. It is not a welfare measure. A related but distinct object is the real purchasing power profile of nominal payments, which deteriorates with inflation (Section 6.2). A third object is the utility-adjusted or certainty-equivalent value, which incorporates inflation risk, liquidity value, default risk, and preference heterogeneity. These three concepts are not interchangeable. MWR < 1 does not automatically imply that annuity purchase is welfare-reducing: for sufficiently risk-averse agents without alternative longevity insurance, the insurance value of the mortality credit can dominate the pricing shortfall. The welfare case against annuitization rests not on pricing alone but on the combination of pricing shortfalls with the demand-side factors documented in Sections 3-5.

Inflation erosion, counterparty risk, illiquidity, and product complexity further reduce the welfare-equivalent value below the nominal MWR. The cumulative effect renders the welfare-equivalent value of a standard nominal annuity for a population-average buyer well below the actuarially fair benchmark, and for consumers with calibrated bequest motives, health risk, or behavioral frictions, non-annuitization is individually rational even under expected utility maximization.

### 6.2 Inflation Risk

Yaari's theorem implicitly assumes a real annuity. Most annuities sold are nominal: at 3 percent inflation, a nominal annuity loses roughly 45 percent of purchasing power by year 20, delivering its weakest protection in late old age when medical costs are highest. Inflation-indexed annuities are rare and expensive; the limited supply of long-duration inflation-linked assets and associated hedging costs constrain insurers, and initial payouts are typically 25-30 percent lower than comparable nominal products (Brown, Mitchell, and Poterba, 2001, 2002).

### 6.3 Counterparty Risk and Product Complexity

An annuity is, in economic substance, an unsecured, illiquid, long-term loan to an insurance company's general account. The collapse of Executive Life Insurance Company of California in 1991 left annuitants receiving only 70 percent of monthly payments for nearly 13 months, though subsequent rehabilitation proceedings improved outcomes for many policyholders. State guaranty associations provide backstops with coverage typically set at $250,000 in present value of annuity benefits, subject to per-individual caps varying by jurisdiction (NOLHGA). Variable annuity separate account assets are structurally distinct from general-account promises: credit risk primarily attaches to the insurer's guarantees and annuitization commitments rather than to segregated investment accounts.

Product complexity compounds counterparty risk. The retail market offers a bewildering array of product types, each with distinct fee structures and regulatory frameworks. A definitional clarification is important: the annuity puzzle concerns longevity insurance products (SPIAs and DIAs), but the retail annuity market is overwhelmingly dominated by products serving primarily as tax-advantaged accumulation or principal-protection vehicles. Total U.S. annuity sales reached a record $434 billion in 2024 (LIMRA), but SPIAs accounted for only approximately $14 billion and DIAs approximately $5 billion -- together representing less than 5 percent of total annuity sales. The market's center of gravity has shifted toward fixed-rate deferred annuities ($153 billion), fixed indexed annuities ($127 billion), and registered index-linked annuities (RILAs, $66 billion). Many consumers are buying annuity products but not purchasing longevity insurance.

Fixed indexed annuities embed the insurer's margin in participation rates, caps, and spreads, with no stated annual fee. Variable annuity fee structures remain particularly layered -- mortality and expense charges, surrender charges, subaccount management fees, and rider charges can produce total annual costs of 1.5 to 4.5 percent in traditional configurations, though fee-based advisory share classes have compressed costs to approximately 0.90 to 1.50 percent. The product complexity literature (Carlin, 2009) establishes that strategic price complexity inhibits comparison shopping. While technology has reduced search costs for immediate annuity contracts, the complexity of indexed, variable, and rider-laden products remains a real friction for the majority of retail buyers.

### 6.4 Distribution Channel Conflicts

The incentive structure of the advisory industry creates systematic biases against annuitization. Assets-under-management advisors have a direct financial interest in discouraging annuitization, since every dollar that flows into an annuity leaves the fee-generating portfolio; commission-based advisors face the opposite incentive, favoring complex, high-commission products. The regulatory environment has evolved: the SEC's Regulation Best Interest (2020) and the NAIC suitability standards have partially eroded the clean AUM-versus-commission dichotomy, and the growth of direct-to-consumer annuity platforms has reduced search costs for some buyers. Nevertheless, conflicted distribution remains a real friction: neither fee-based nor commission-based incentive structures are reliably aligned with client welfare, and the advisory industry's structural biases continue to influence the annuitization decision for the majority of retirees who receive financial advice.

If low annuity demand were driven primarily by market imperfections rather than by deeper preferences, we would expect significant variation across institutional contexts. The cross-cultural evidence provides instructive institutional variation. Table 2 summarizes the major cases.

The UK case provides the strongest quasi-experimental evidence. For decades, Britain appeared to be the great exception to the annuity puzzle: retirees annuitized their DC pension savings at rates dramatically higher than elsewhere. What appeared to be robust consumer demand was, in substantial part, an artifact of compulsion: UK tax law effectively required annuity purchase, with flexible drawdown available only to wealthier individuals. The 2015 pension freedoms constitute the closest approximation to a natural experiment, though several simultaneous changes limit clean causal interpretation. The reforms removed the annuity requirement while introducing flexi-access drawdown with no income floor. The 2013 Retail Distribution Review had restructured the advice market, and the near-zero rate environment (2015-2021) independently suppressed annuity attractiveness. Quarterly sales fell from 74,092 to 18,243, a decline of approximately 75 percent. By 2018/19, only 11 percent of pension plans accessed for the first time were used to purchase an annuity (FCA, 2019). Annual annuity sales rebounded to 89,600 contracts in 2024 (ABI) as rates rose, suggesting pricing partially explains the initial decline's depth. Nevertheless, the directional finding -- removing compulsion substantially reduced take-up -- is robust.

The TIAA-CREF case tells a parallel story in what should be among the most favorable environments for annuitization: low-cost products with money's-worth ratios well above the retail market average, a trusted institutional brand with decades of reputation, and a participant population of highly educated university and nonprofit employees -- precisely the demographic that financial literacy research identifies as best equipped for complex financial decisions. Yet Brown, Poterba, and Richardson (2022) documented that the share electing life-annuity income at the required minimum distribution age fell from 41 percent to 6 percent over the 2000-2018 period. The decline reflects shifting institutional norms: TIAA historically defaulted participants into annuitized payout structures, and as the system moved toward greater participant choice and lump-sum access, annuitization declined sharply. The TIAA case is particularly informative because it controls for many supply-side frictions -- pricing, product complexity, counterparty risk, and distribution channel conflicts -- that plague the retail market, isolating the role of preferences, defaults, and behavioral factors.

Australia provides a cross-sectional observation rather than a natural experiment: compulsory superannuation has accumulated AUD 3.6 trillion with no annuitization mandate. Lifetime annuity products exist and are offered by reputable insurers, yet they constitute a negligible fraction of superannuation assets. The dominant vehicle is the account-based pension (ABP), a regulated drawdown product -- retirees are not simply holding idle wealth but are drawing from structured income vehicles that preserve flexibility and bequest capacity. The means-tested Age Pension further complicates the picture: because annuity income counts under the income test, purchasing a lifetime annuity can reduce Age Pension entitlements, creating an implicit tax on annuitization for retirees near the means-test threshold. The tax treatment of lifetime annuities was substantially improved only in 2017, and the Retirement Income Covenant (enacted 2021; effective July 2022), mandating that trustees formulate longevity-risk strategies, implicitly acknowledges that the market has not spontaneously generated adequate longevity products -- a fact that complicates the preference-based explanation, though it does not refute it.

Singapore's CPF LIFE, mandatory from 2013, achieved high compliance as a national longevity insurance scheme (Fong, Mitchell, and Koh, 2011). Germany's Riester pension has seen stagnating enrollment growth since the late 2000s, despite generous means-tested subsidies, and has been subject to sustained criticism regarding costs and product design (Borsch-Supan, Coppola, and Reil-Held, 2012). Whether declining enrollment reflects resistance to annuitization specifically, or dissatisfaction with product costs, is not firmly established. Scandinavian hybrid systems achieve high annuitization through institutional defaults, further illustrating the sensitivity of outcomes to institutional design.

The cross-cultural evidence does not support a uniform narrative. Chile's high voluntary annuitization rate (over 60 percent choosing annuities over programmed withdrawals; Illanes and Padi, 2019) demonstrates that institutional design -- including structured choice architecture, regulatory oversight, and limited alternatives -- can produce substantially higher take-up than the Anglo-Saxon cases would predict. The Netherlands achieves near-universal annuitization through collective occupational arrangements rather than individual retail choice. These cases complicate any claim that low voluntary annuitization reflects deep and invariant preferences; institutional design, choice architecture, and the available menu of alternatives substantially condition outcomes.

The overall pattern: when annuitization is mandated or embedded in institutional defaults, compliance is high and outcomes appear stable; when the mandate is removed and attractive alternatives become available (UK), demand falls substantially; when purely voluntary in a retail market context (US, Australia), take-up is low. Whether the low-take-up cases reflect stable preferences, supply-side failures, behavioral frictions, or some combination remains an open question that the cross-cultural evidence alone cannot resolve.

### 6.6 Interpreting the Cross-Cultural Evidence

The cross-cultural record admits three interpretations, each with distinct policy implications. First, behavioral biases (loss aversion, complexity aversion, passivity) may overwhelm rational choice once mandates are removed. The FCA's Retirement Outcomes Review (2018) evidence that many post-2015 decisions reflect passivity rather than deliberate optimization supports this interpretation -- large numbers of retirees leaving funds in cash drawdown earning near-zero returns, failing to compare products, or not seeking advice. This interpretive possibility should be taken seriously, as it would imply that removing annuitization mandates reduces welfare for at least some retirees.

Second, the structural channels (pre-existing annuitization, bequest motives, health risk) independently justify low annuitization on rational grounds for the majority of households. When the structural channels predict low annuitization, preference is the more parsimonious explanation.

These interpretations are not mutually exclusive. For retirees who are single, childless, healthy, wealthy, and possess low bequest motives, the structural channels provide weaker justification, and behavioral factors may account for welfare-reducing under-annuitization. This subpopulation is not trivially small: approximately 15-20 percent of retirees are single without children, and even if only 5-10 percent of all retirees fall into this category, it potentially represents millions of households. The aggregate positive finding should be distinguished from the heterogeneous welfare question (Section 8.2).

Third, low take-up in the UK, US, and Australia is consistent with the kin-directed investment motives documented in Section 3.3. The annuity, which converts bequeathable wealth into a non-transferable consumption flow, is in tension with the kin-directed preferences evolutionary theory suggests. However, the high-annuitization cases (Chile, Netherlands, Denmark) demonstrate that institutional design can produce high annuitization even where bequest motives are presumably present, suggesting that while bequest motives persistently depress voluntary retail demand, they are not an insuperable barrier when the institutional framework is well designed. The cross-cultural evidence supports a conditional interpretation: low voluntary annuitization reflects the interaction of bequest motives, supply-side frictions, and behavioral factors with institutional design, rather than preferences invariant to institutional context.

Second, nearly every study reviewed finds that realistic modifications reduce predicted annuitization, often dramatically, though Peijnenburg et al. (2016) find that full annuitization remains approximately optimal within their unified framework. Third, several studies find that specific channels or channel combinations can generate very low predicted demand, though the strength of the result varies with parameterization and model structure.

It is important to note where the literature disagrees, and to do so with specificity. Pashchenko (2013) is particularly relevant because she explicitly nests multiple impediments and performs sequential relative-importance exercises. Her benchmark calibration with all impediments (preannuitized wealth, minimum purchase requirement, illiquid housing, bequest motives) still predicts approximately 20 percent annuity market participation, against roughly 5 percent in the data -- a fourfold overprediction. She explicitly notes that no single friction is sufficient and that the model does not fully close the gap even with all frictions jointly. Peijnenburg et al. (2016) reach a similar conclusion, titling their paper 'The Annuity Puzzle Remains a Puzzle.' Inkmann, Lopes, and Michaelides (2011), using UK data, find that bequest motives and pre-existing income rationalize low demand for most households but that the puzzle persists for a minority.

This tension is genuine. The present survey reaches a more decisive conclusion by synthesizing across modeling frameworks: Lockwood (2012) approximates observed ownership with bequests plus purchase frictions; Reichling and Smetters (2015) generate zero demand through stochastic mortality alone; Hu and Scott (2007) generate zero demand under CPT. But Pashchenko's joint model still overpredicts by a factor of four. Two interpretations: the channels she omits (prospect theory, survival pessimism, the full pricing wedge) may account for the residual, or the divergence may reflect differences in calibration and model structure. Resolution requires structural models incorporating all channels simultaneously (Section 8.3).

### 7.2 Interaction Effects: Why the Channels Compound

A critical feature of the literature obscured by channel-by-channel treatment is that the explanatory mechanisms interact multiplicatively. Pre-existing annuitization through Social Security means the marginal utility of additional annuity income is low; bequest motives mean the cost of annuitization -- forfeited bequeathable wealth -- is high; health expenditure risk makes liquidity precious; and supply-side frictions render pricing unfavorable. When these channels operate simultaneously, the residual benefit of annuitization may be zero or negative.

The interaction between bequest motives and loading costs illustrates the compounding mechanism. Lockwood (2012) showed that bequests plus purchase frictions reduce predicted ownership from 61 percent to 4-5 percent. At realistic loads of 15-20 percent (Mitchell et al., 1999), WTP among consumers with calibrated bequest motives drops to zero. The load eliminates the already-small surplus that remained after accounting for bequests.

A parallel compounding operates between stochastic mortality and bequest motives. Reichling and Smetters (2015) demonstrated that stochastic mortality alone, when correlated with medical costs, can drive optimal demand to zero without bequest motives. Adding bequests only strengthens the case, because it introduces an additional cost to a product whose insurance value has already been undermined. A third compounding occurs between behavioral factors and structural channels: survival pessimism reduces the perceived mortality credit (the primary source of annuity value), while loss aversion increases the perceived cost of premium forfeiture. The channels form layers of resistance, each filtering out additional potential purchasers, and their joint operation is consistent with the low annuitization levels observed.

### 7.3 How Much of the Puzzle Does Each Channel Explain?

Any attempt to assign precise quantitative weights must be acknowledged as imprecise, both because channels interact nonlinearly and because different studies use different modeling assumptions and calibration strategies. Nevertheless, a rough decomposition is instructive.

Pre-existing annuitization narrows the puzzle to a fraction of the population. When Social Security and DB pension wealth are properly included, the typical retiree is already 50-75 percent annuitized (Dushi and Webb, 2004; Lockwood, 2012). The marginal value of additional annuitization is therefore low for most of the wealth distribution. The puzzle, properly measured, applies at most to the top 10-20 percent -- households with sufficient non-annuitized financial wealth to make voluntary purchase meaningful.

Bequest motives close most of the remaining gap. Among households with meaningful annuitizable wealth, Lockwood's (2012) calibration reduces predicted ownership from 61 percent to 4-5 percent, with bequests functioning as a luxury good at the top (Lockwood, 2018). Health expenditure risk and stochastic mortality reinforce independently: De Nardi, French, and Jones (2010) showed that medical expense risk is a key driver of elderly precautionary saving, while Reichling and Smetters (2015) showed that stochastic mortality correlated with medical costs can drive demand to zero even without bequests.

Behavioral factors predict low demand through different mechanisms. Under CPT with narrow framing, Hu and Scott (2007) find predicted demand falls to zero, though this depends on the parameterization. Brown et al. (2008) demonstrated that framing alone swings demand by 51 percentage points, and the prevailing market framing is the one associated with low demand. Supply-side pricing shortfalls (MWR 0.80-0.85 against population mortality) further reduce welfare gains from annuitization.

An important methodological caution applies to any claim based on cross-paper synthesis. Many channels are not independent and cannot be stacked without double-counting risk: bequests, public care aversion, and means-tested transfers all affect late-life saving through related state-contingent marginal utility logic; medical expense risk is often already embedded in the calibrations used to identify bequest motives; loads and adverse selection are not separable from product choice. Moreover, prospect theory is not a friction layered on top of EU; it replaces the preference representation entirely. The more defensible formulation is: multiple competing modeling frameworks can generate low predicted annuitization under empirically plausible parameterizations, and no framework predicts high voluntary immediate-annuity demand once realistic institutional features are incorporated. Quantitative results vary across models, outcome definitions, and calibration targets -- as the Pashchenko (2013) residual gap illustrates.

This is the author's synthesis of the accumulated evidence; several individual studies reach more cautious conclusions. The aggregate finding should be distinguished from the heterogeneous normative question: even if the puzzle dissolves at the population level, some households may lose welfare from non-annuitization (Section 8.2).


## 8. Remaining Questions and Directions for Future Research

The dissolution of the aggregate puzzle does not mean that all questions about annuitization have been answered.

### 8.1 The Deferred Income Annuity Puzzle

The dissolution argument applies most directly to classic immediate life annuities. The strongest residual challenge concerns deferred income annuities (DIAs), including qualified longevity annuity contracts (QLACs). These products address several of the most powerful objections: the consumer retains full access to wealth until a specified advanced age, typically 80 or 85, preserving liquidity during the period when health expenditure risk is most acute, while providing longevity insurance precisely at the ages when outliving resources is most threatening. The premium is substantially smaller as a fraction of total wealth than for an immediate annuity, reducing the bequest sacrifice. The SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022 raised the per-person QLAC contribution limit to $200,000, removing the previous 25-percent-of-balance constraint.

Yet demand remains near zero. This fact warrants careful analysis because the structural channels documented in Sections 3-6 apply with substantially reduced force to DIAs. Pre-existing annuitization is less relevant because DIA payouts would supplement, not duplicate, Social Security income at advanced ages. Bequest motives apply with reduced force because the DIA premium is a modest fraction of total wealth. Health expenditure risk is directly addressed by the product's design: liquidity is preserved during the years when catastrophic health costs are most likely. Pricing remains a friction -- Wettstein et al. (2021) report DIA money's worth ratios around 50 cents per dollar of premium in nominal terms, though the insurance value on a utility basis is substantially higher -- but the QLAC's location within a tax-advantaged retirement account mitigates some of the pricing objection. The $200,000 cap itself limits the product for upper-wealth households.

The most plausible explanations for near-zero DIA demand are behavioral, operating through several reinforcing channels. Loss aversion operates against a product that requires forfeiting current wealth for payments contingent on survival to age 85 -- the "loss" (premium paid) is certain and immediate, while the "gain" (income stream) is contingent and remote. Survival pessimism (O'Dea and Sturrock, 2023) reduces the perceived value of a product whose entire benefit is conditional on reaching advanced age; given that individuals underestimate survival probabilities by 15-25 percentage points, the subjective probability of collecting DIA benefits is substantially lower than the actuarial probability. The cognitive challenge is substantial: evaluating a 20-year irreversible commitment based on survival probabilities that individuals systematically underestimate. Distribution channel frictions compound the behavioral obstacles: financial advisors compensated through assets under management have little incentive to recommend a product that removes assets from the fee-generating portfolio, while the modest commissions on QLACs relative to indexed annuities create weak incentives for commission-based advisors. The DIA puzzle is the most plausible candidate for a genuine residual anomaly and the strongest test case for whether behavioral biases constitute a policy-relevant welfare problem rather than a confirmatory gloss on structurally justified behavior.

### 8.2 Heterogeneity in Optimal Annuitization

The dissolution of the aggregate puzzle does not imply that no individual household would benefit from additional annuitization. Retirees are heterogeneous along every relevant dimension: wealth, pre-existing annuity income, bequest motives, health status, risk preferences, family structure, and cognitive ability. The subpopulation for whom the welfare case is strongest includes: single individuals without dependent children (approximately 15-20 percent of retirees); those in good health with above-median expected longevity; those with financial wealth in the upper quartile, where pre-existing annuitization through Social Security may cover only one-third of total wealth (Lockwood, 2012); and those reporting low bequest motives (approximately 25 percent of elderly single households report no bequest motive; Kopczuk and Lupton, 2007). The intersection is not trivially small: even a conservative estimate suggests potentially millions of American households whose structural justification for non-annuitization is weak and for whom behavioral factors may account for welfare-reducing under-annuitization.

The welfare question for this subpopulation cannot be dissolved by appeal to the aggregate evidence. Future research should provide quantitative estimates of the welfare loss from non-annuitization for these households, using the building blocks provided by Lockwood (2012) and related structural models. The organizing question is not "is there a puzzle?" but "for which households, under what conditions, would additional annuitization improve welfare, and by how much?"

### 8.3 Directions for Future Research

Four directions merit emphasis. First, structural models jointly incorporating pre-existing annuitization, bequest motives, health risk, stochastic mortality, and realistic pricing are needed. The divergence between this survey's synthesis and Peijnenburg et al. (2016) can only be resolved by models allowing all channels to operate simultaneously.

Second, DIA and QLAC demand warrants specific analysis as the strongest test of whether behavioral biases generate welfare losses in the annuitization domain.

Third, the optimal default literature (Carroll et al., 2009; Beshears et al., 2014) has direct implications. If the UK post-2015 experience reflects default effects rather than informed preference, optimal default design for retirement income may be more productive than information provision or tax incentives.

Fourth, investigation of Social Security delay as the dominant annuitization mechanism deserves priority. For many American retirees, delaying benefits from age 62 to 70 is functionally equivalent to purchasing additional annuity income -- up to 8 percent per year in delayed retirement credits for cohorts born after 1942, on an inflation-indexed (CPI-W), survivor-benefit-eligible annuity backed by the federal government. This creates a tension with the present survey's broader argument: the same bequest and liquidity arguments that justify non-purchase of private annuities should, in principle, apply to the delay decision.

Several qualifications are necessary. The actuarial fairness of delay is heterogeneous: because mortality differs substantially by socioeconomic status, the "deal" is more favorable for longer-lived groups and less favorable for shorter-lived groups. Many households cannot finance the "premium" (foregone benefits) because they lack liquid wealth or earnings to bridge the gap. For married couples, the delay decision is closer to a joint-and-survivor annuity purchase (affecting survivor benefits), adding complexity. The COLA uses CPI-W, not a retiree-specific inflation index. Nevertheless, for households with sufficient bridging resources and above-average life expectancy, delay offers substantially better pricing than private markets and full inflation indexation, making the welfare case robust to the frictions that undermine private annuity purchase. This suggests that the conditions under which annuitization is welfare-improving are product-specific and pricing-dependent -- a nuance that the aggregate "retire the puzzle" framing should not obscure.

### 8.4 Limitations

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the cross-cultural evidence does not permit clean causal identification: the seven institutional contexts examined involve multiple simultaneous differences, and the high-annuitization cases (Chile, Netherlands) demonstrate that institutional design can produce outcomes substantially different from the low-take-up pattern. Second, the quantitative match between Lockwood's (2012) calibration and observed ownership rates depends on specific bequest parameterizations that, while empirically defensible, are not the only defensible calibrations. Third, the behavioral evidence (Sections 5.1-5.5) documents systematic departures from expected utility that, while confirming the positive prediction of low annuitization, may simultaneously identify welfare-reducing biases for specific subpopulations -- a possibility that the aggregate dissolution thesis should not be read as dismissing. Fourth, the supply-side analysis relies primarily on US data from Mitchell et al. (1999) and Wettstein et al. (2021); the dominance of fixed indexed annuities and income rider products in current sales represents a market development not fully addressed in academic pricing literature. Fifth, the DIA/QLAC puzzle (Section 8.1) represents a genuine residual anomaly that the channels documented here do not fully explain. Sixth, the pre-existing annuitization argument (50-75 percent of retiree wealth in annuitized form) relies primarily on cohorts with substantial DB pension coverage; as the long-run shift from DB to DC continues, the annuitized share for future retirees may decline, potentially strengthening the case for voluntary annuitization. Seventh, the welfare conclusions are framework-dependent: this survey implicitly adopts expected utility with calibrated parameters as the normative benchmark, but alternative behavioral welfare frameworks (Bernheim and Rangel, 2009) may yield different assessments, particularly for the subpopulation identified in Section 8.2.


## 9. Conclusion

The annuity puzzle has occupied household finance researchers for six decades. The accumulated evidence demonstrates that the aggregate puzzle -- the gap between Yaari's (1965) full-annuitization prediction and the observed 3-6 percent ownership rate for classic immediate life annuities -- was always more about the model than about the people. The Yaari result assumes a consumer who has no family to provide for, faces no health shocks, inhabits a frictionless market, and processes decisions with the frame-invariance of the expected utility axioms.

The case for retiring the Yaari-benchmark puzzle framing operates through three mutually reinforcing layers. First, the Yaari result rests on a conjunction of nine restrictive assumptions, each empirically false, and under cumulative prospect theory with standard parameters, predicted demand falls to zero (Hu and Scott, 2007). Second, corrected inputs converge on observed levels: pre-existing annuitization places the typical retiree at 50-75 percent annuitized (Dushi and Webb, 2004), bequest motives combined with purchase frictions reduce predicted ownership to approximately 4-5 percent (Lockwood, 2012), and health expenditure risk correlated with stochastic mortality can drive demand to zero (Reichling and Smetters, 2015). Third, supply-side frictions render real products far less attractive than their theoretical counterparts (Mitchell et al., 1999; Finkelstein and Poterba, 2002).

Not all researchers share this assessment. Pashchenko's (2013) structural model incorporating multiple channels still overpredicts participation by a factor of four, and Peijnenburg et al. (2016) find full annuitization approximately optimal in their calibrated framework, titling their paper 'The Annuity Puzzle Remains a Puzzle.' The divergence reflects genuine methodological disagreement about whether channels should be evaluated within unified models or synthesized across frameworks; the present survey adopts the latter approach, on the ground that no single model incorporates all empirically relevant channels simultaneously. Harmonization of these approaches remains an important open task.

Retiring the puzzle framing does not mean retiring the research program. Quite the opposite: the productive research agenda that emerges is richer than the one the puzzle framing supports. The deferred income annuity puzzle represents the strongest residual test case: a product that addresses the liquidity and health-risk objections and yet attracts near-zero demand. The dissolution of the aggregate puzzle does not imply that annuities are valueless, nor that all households are making welfare-optimal decisions. Partial annuitization remains welfare-improving for some households -- particularly those who are single, healthy, wealthy, and possess low bequest motives. Moreover, low annuitization can be simultaneously individually rational given current products and evidence of welfare losses from insurance-market incompleteness.

The relevant questions going forward include: for which households would additional annuitization improve welfare? What default and menu design in DC decumulation would best serve heterogeneous retirees? Can institutional pooling or public provision offer higher-money's-worth longevity insurance than retail markets? How does the annuitization decision interact with LTC risk and Medicaid design? These questions direct research toward the specific populations, product designs, and institutional arrangements where the gap between optimal and actual annuitization is largest and most consequential -- an agenda that the "retire the puzzle" framing is designed to facilitate, not foreclose.

The broader methodological lesson may be the most important. The annuity puzzle is a case study in the consequences of treating a fragile theoretical benchmark as a robust empirical prediction. The full-annuitization result held only under a restrictive conjunction of assumptions -- nine restrictive assumptions, each empirically false. When "puzzle" becomes the organizing concept for a research program, it biases inquiry toward explaining away observed behavior rather than questioning the benchmark. Six decades of research have progressively demonstrated that the gap between theory and behavior reflects the limitations of the original benchmark rather than a failure of consumer decision-making. The field would benefit from applying this methodological lesson more broadly: when a theoretical prediction requires a knife-edge conjunction of counterfactual assumptions, the failure of the prediction is not a puzzle -- it is the expected outcome.
